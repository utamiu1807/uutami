üìò Executive Summary

The analysis of CIF Import Values between 2023 and 2025 reveals how U.S. trade patterns evolved amid shifting global conditions and policy reforms. From 2023 to 2024, total imports increased by 5.9%, marking steady recovery after COVID. For 2025, we cannot conclude the trend because we only have data until August 2025. There are 232 countries were analyzed. When ranked by import value, the top 30 countries display a relatively stable pattern over time, indicating that the concentration of trade among leading partners has remained consistent year over year. However, there is a noticeable gap between the three largest importers and the rest of the countries.

The comparison between 2024 and 2025 CIF import values highlights how trade policies reshaped U.S. import dynamics. In 2024, import volumes reflected a stable, pre-tariff environment dominated by major partners such as China, Mexico, and Canada. By 2025, as reciprocal tariffs and selective trade deals took effect, the composition of top-importing countries began to shift: nations granted trade deals or exempt from tariffs generally maintained or increased their export values, while those hit by tariffs experienced noticeable declines.

China‚Äôs Slower Growth: In 2025, China‚Äôs import growth decelerated notably. Among the three leading trading partners, China stands out as the only one without a formal trade deal with the Trump administration, which likely contributed to its slower performance.

Exempt Economies ‚Äî Switzerland and Singapore: Within the top 20 importing countries, only Switzerland and Singapore were exempt from the reciprocal tariff measures. This exemption helps explain Switzerland‚Äôs early upward trend in import activity during 2025, even though the available data only extends through August.

Germany‚Äôs Decline: Germany, ranked as the fourth-largest importer in 2024, showed a decline in 2025. Like China, Germany did not secure a trade agreement with the Trump administration, and this absence appears to have weighed on its import levels.

üß™ Methodology

3.1 Data Sources

This analysis uses U.S. import data (CIF values) covering the period 2023‚Äì2025. The dataset includes:

  • Monthly CIF import values by country
  • HS codes at different levels of detail (HTS 2 and HTS 4 used in this project)
  • Country-level trade information for 232 partners
  • Custom policy labels indicating whether each country is in a Deal, Tariffed, or Not-Hit group under the Trump reciprocal tariff policy in 2025

3.2 Industry Sector Classification (API-Based)

Industry sector labels in this project are generated through API-based metadata retrieval, not through OpenAI or other LLM classification. External APIs are used to obtain official HS metadata, including:

  • HS product descriptions
  • HS chapter and subchapter information
  • Sector or industry group mapping (e.g., Machinery & Electronics, Chemicals & Plastics, Transportation & Vehicles)

These fields are merged into the CIF dataset by matching on HS codes. As a result, the industry classification is aligned with established trade-taxonomy standards and remains fully reproducible. No AI-generated labels are used in this step.

3.3 Data Cleaning and Integration

Before analysis and visualization, several preprocessing steps are applied:

  • Standardizing country codes and names to a consistent format
  • Filtering to HTS 2 and HTS 4 levels for tractable and meaningful aggregation
  • Handling missing values and removing obviously erroneous records
  • Aggregating CIF values by country, year, and industry sector
  • Calculating percent changes between 2023‚Äì2024 and 2024‚Äì2025 for each country and industry

3.4 Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

EDA is conducted in Python using pandas, matplotlib, and seaborn. Key steps include:

  • Ranking countries by total CIF import value to identify top partners
  • Comparing trends across 2023‚Äì2025 to capture recovery and disruption patterns
  • Grouping countries into policy categories (Deal, Tariffed, Not-Hit) and comparing their import trajectories
  • Aggregating CIF values by industry to understand sectoral concentration and shifts
  • Building correlation and regression models to test whether policy variables help explain changes in import values

3.5 Visualization

Static and interactive visualizations are used to communicate the results:

  • Bar and line charts to show trends for the Top 20 and Top 30 import partners over time
  • Side-by-side bar charts to compare 2024 vs 2025 CIF values by country, with policy status highlighted
  • Interactive choropleth maps to display dominant industries by country and policy group
  • Summary tables to show the distribution of countries across Deal, Tariffed, and Not-Hit categories

All figures and maps displayed on this page are generated directly from the cleaned dataset and Python EDA notebooks linked at the bottom of the page.
